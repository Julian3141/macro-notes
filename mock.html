The Ramsey
Household

Intertemporal choice, lifetime utility, and the Keynes-Ramsey rule of optimal consumption.

Motivation

Analyse the macroeconomic dynamics over time:

  • What are consequences of epidemic shocks?
  • How does capital accumulation affect the distribution of income?

The model features intertemporal choices and economic shocks. Limits: no heterogeneity; no shifts in employment.

1.1Households

Quick actions:

1.1.1Households: Setup

Mass of one identical and infinitely lived households populate the economy. Each household supplies labor inelastically, holds wealth, and chooses a consumption path that maximizes lifetime utility.

  • $x(i)$ denotes income of household $i \in [0,1]$. Aggregate income: $X = \int_0^1 x(i)\, di$.
    • Identical households: $x(i) = \bar x \;\forall\, i \implies X = \int_0^1 \bar x\, di = \bar x$.
  • $L$ denotes household members who all supply one unit of time. Aggregate labor supply $= L \cdot 1$.
    • $L$ is fully inelastic (vertical line in $w$–$L$ graph) and could in principle change over time, i.e. $L(t)$.
  • $a(t)$ denotes wealth, which is lent to firms.
  • Quantities per household coincide with aggregate quantities.
  • Household income (= aggregate income): $X(t) = w(t) L + r(t) a(t)$, where $r$ is the interest rate, $w$ the wage rate, $t \in [0, \infty]$.

Every household maximizes its lifetime utility:

$$U = \int_0^\infty u\big(C(t)\big) \cdot e^{-\rho t}\, dt \quad \text{with } \rho > 0$$

where $\rho$ is the time preference rate, $u$ is instantaneous utility, $C$ is consumption. The higher $\rho$, the faster $e^{-\rho t}$ converges to zero.

1.1.2Households: Instantaneous utility

Households have the instantaneous utility function (instantaneous meaning that the function describes satisfaction at a single moment, not aggregated over a period):

$$u\big(C(t)\big) = \frac{C(t)^{1-\sigma} - 1}{1-\sigma} \quad \text{with } \sigma > 0$$

Alternative formulation (since $\lim_{\sigma \to 1} u(C) = \ln C$):

$$u(C) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{C^{1-\sigma}}{1-\sigma} & \text{for } \sigma \neq 1 \\ \ln C & \text{for } \sigma = 1 \end{cases}$$
  • $u$ is compatible with steady-state growth.
  • The lifetime utility (welfare) function implies CIES: constant intertemporal elasticity of substitution.
  • Elasticity of marginal utility w.r.t. $C$ is constant: $$\eta_{u'(C), C} = \frac{u''(C) \cdot C}{u'(C)} = \frac{-\sigma C^{-\sigma-1} \cdot C}{C^{-\sigma}} = -\sigma < 0$$

The parameter $\sigma$ is a measure of concavity of the utility function: the higher $\sigma$, the more concave $u(C)$.

Example: Lifetime utility over three periods

Which consumption profile is preferable: a smooth one (red) or a fluctuating one (blue)?

  • The red consumption profile is preferable as it smooths consumption over time compared to the blue profile.
  • Marginal utility is given by $u'(C) = C^{-\sigma}$ and is therefore decreasing in $C$. The more concave the utility function, the higher the incentive for consumption smoothing.
  • In the blue profile $C = 3$ at $t = 3$ and $C = 1$ at $t = 1$. Thus $u'(3) < u'(1)$. Decreasing consumption at $t = 3$ by 1 and increasing it at $t = 1$ by 1 raises lifetime utility.

1.1.3Households: Dynamic optimization

Each household solves the following problem to optimize welfare / lifetime utility:

$$\max_{\{C(t)\}} \int_0^\infty \frac{C(t)^{1-\sigma} - 1}{1-\sigma}\, e^{-\rho t}\, dt$$ $$\text{s.t.} \quad \dot a(t) = w(t) L + r(t) a(t) - C(t)$$
  • Initial condition: $a(0) = a_0$.
  • Terminal condition (no-Ponzi-game): $\lim_{t \to \infty} e^{-R(t)} a(t) \geq 0$.
  • One control variable $C(t)$, one state variable $a(t)$.
  • The control variable is freely chosen and could in principle jump; the state variable is more inert (träge).

We set up the Hamiltonian for this optimization problem:

$$H := \underbrace{\frac{C^{1-\sigma} - 1}{1-\sigma}}_{\text{instantaneous objective}} + \underbrace{\lambda}_{\substack{\text{current-value}\\\text{shadow price}}} \cdot \underbrace{(wL + ra - C)}_{\text{RHS of dynamic constraint}}$$

First-order conditions:

$$\frac{\partial H}{\partial C} = C^{-\sigma} - \lambda = 0 \tag{3}$$ $$\dot \lambda = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial a} + \rho \lambda = -\lambda r + \rho \lambda \tag{4}$$ $$\lim_{t \to \infty} \underbrace{e^{-\rho t}\lambda(t)}_{\text{present-value shadow price}} a(t) = 0 \tag{TVC}$$

The TVC states that the present value of wealth, valued at the shadow price $\lambda(t)$, must approach zero as time goes to infinity.

1.1.4The Keynes-Ramsey Rule

Key Result: Keynes-Ramsey Rule

Optimal consumption obeys the Keynes-Ramsey rule:

$$\hat C := \frac{\dot C}{C} = \frac{1}{\sigma}(r - \rho)$$

Consumption growth $\hat C > 0$ whenever $r > \rho$.

  • The interest rate $r$ is the premium the household receives for reducing $C$ today by 1 unit and increasing it tomorrow. $r$ is the opportunity cost of consumption today; higher $r$ gives an incentive to postpone $C$.
  • The time preference rate $\rho$ is the premium the household demands to be marginally indifferent between $C$ today and tomorrow. High $\rho$ captures impatience and reduces saving.
  • The intertemporal elasticity of substitution, governed by $\sigma$, captures willingness to substitute consumption across time. Small $\sigma$ implies that $u'(C)$ does not fall strongly as $C$ rises, so the household is highly willing to substitute intertemporally.

If $r > \rho$, it is rational to postpone consumption into the future; the smaller $\sigma$, the stronger this incentive unfolds.

Derivation of the Keynes-Ramsey Rule

Take equation (3), rearrange and differentiate (chain rule):

$$\frac{d}{dt}(C^{-\sigma}) = \frac{d}{dt}(\lambda) \iff -\sigma C^{-\sigma-1} \dot C = \dot \lambda$$

Plug in (4):

$$-\sigma C^{-\sigma-1} \dot C = -\lambda r + \rho \lambda$$

Divide by $C^{-\sigma} = \lambda$ (from equation 3):

$$\frac{-\sigma C^{-\sigma-1}\dot C}{C^{-\sigma}} = \frac{-\lambda r + \rho \lambda}{\lambda}$$

Rearranging:

$$-\sigma C^{-1} \dot C = -r + \rho$$ $$\hat C = \frac{\dot C}{C} = \frac{1}{\sigma}(r - \rho)$$

An alternative method is to take $\ln$ of equation (3) and differentiate.

Macroeconomic equilibrium remark

Steady state implies $c := C/Y$ remains constant, hence $\hat C = \hat Y$ in the long run. The KRR demonstrates that $r$ plays a prominent role in determining $\hat C$ and thus $\hat Y$. Since $r = F'(K) - \delta$, anything that affects the marginal product of capital impacts the growth rate. If $F'(K) > 0$ and $F''(K) < 0$, MPK diminishes as capital is accumulated until $r = \rho$. Economic growth is then only possible if $F'(K) - \delta > \rho$.
